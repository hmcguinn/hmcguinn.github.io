On Colorblindness

Some of my experiences and thoughts on colorblindess.

People who know me know I'm colorblind. Once a week, I roll up to school wearing one color head-to-toe (usually blue). My finsta name is Anomalous Trichromat. I've embraced not being able to see color as a part of my identity. All the men in my immediate family are colorblind--my mom is a carrier and my dad is colorblind. I've known I'm colorblind since I was 8 or 9. A math test once asked me to group different color plates into three groups... I only grouped into two. I did not get the question right and only got credit after explaining the misunderstanding to my teacher.

Colorblindness is fascinating [1]. For such a widespread condition, there is surprisingly little awareness of it. 8-10% of men and ~3% of women are colorblind. Yet, when I talk to mention my colorblindness to people it is almost always met with fascination. "What color is that" is invariably the next question someone asks after they find out I'm colorblind. Colorblindness is about unique perception of reality.

With colorblindness, two people can stand next to each other and experience two unique realities. Even with two trichromats--do they see the same colors? Is there one, objective reality that everyone experiences? In terms of color--and vision--there is not. We perceive the world in different ways yet still experience the same world. Two people see the same apple. Yet, they perceive its redness differently. A colorblind man sees the apple less vibrantly but is his perception of the apple less valid? We each have a unique perception--yet equally valid--perception of the world. Debating which is "more" right is a useless exercise.

Colorblindness is a reminder that we all have unique perceptions of the world. Our "self" is the product of past experiences and actions and cannot be separated from our current perception of the world. Like a lense, our past forms a filter through which we see the present. No lense is more or less correct, each filters differently.

I wish I could see the world the "right" way. I wish I could see flowers and trees and paintings in all their vibrancy. I wish I wasn't suprised when someone points out the red berries on a bush. Enchroma makes glasses that are designed to "bring vibrant color to your world." I tried a pair and was genuinely surprised at the difference they made but not blown away. The glasses made everything slightly more saturated just like turning on an instagram filter. The most WOW moment I had was when driving past an office building on Lynhaven parkway. The red-brick building, one I had been driving by my whole life, suddenly was entirely different. The building did not change. The only change was the lense (literally) through which my perception of life was filtered.

Describing color is HARD. On computers, we have the RGB (#99badd) system. Each red/green/blue is assigned a value between 0 and 255. This is where the 16 million colors (24-bit) that you hear about in advertising comes from. Printers use a similar system with magenta/yellow/cyan and black. Standardizing and describing colors is such big business that Pantone has an annual revenue of fifty-million dollars. Can you imagine a color you haven't seen?

The philosophical reasoning behind this post is the idea of qualia. Qualia, according to Wikipedia, is "individual instances of subjective conscious experience". Alongside our uniquely personal experience of observing a painting, we as humans share a collective experience of that painting. Describing our personal experience is a much harder task than relating to the shared experience of a friend who also sees that same painting. My perception of a colorful painting is then viewed through the lense of me not being able to experience all the colors the "proper" way. I physically see a and feelings different painting than someone standing next to me. Yet, our shared experience of that painting can be similar. It can evoke the same emotions

Since thinking more about my colorblindness and talking about it with people. I don't know. I'm really not sure where to go with this.

[1] I use the term colorblindness and color deficiency interchangeably. While not technically correct, color deficiency is colloquially referred to as colorblindness. More correctly, only people with achromatic vision are truly "blind" to colors and see in black and white. Achromats are extremely rare.
